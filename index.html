Analyzing Aircraft Incident Data from 1995-2016

Info Visualization P5 by Yuan Zhang and Hyunjae Lee

For any mode of transportation, there are always both good and bad years.
Using the dataset provided, we wanted to explore potential patterns in
aircraft accidents over a 21 year period ending 2016.

We started out by analyzing yearly frequency of accidents for any patterns.
Our expectation prior to creating the first visualization was that we
would see a stable level of global incidents - mainly because we
anticipated the growth in the airline industry and popularity of flying
would proportionally increase the number of accidents while improvements in
technology would offset some of the growth.

Below in the table of fatalities since 1995, we can see that there are only
five good years in which there are less than 100 deaths; in bad years such
as 1996, there were up to 1013 deaths worldwide.

Although there is an appearance of wide variation in terms of fatality
count, we can clearly see that that the period leading up to 2010 has a
consistently higher death count than the period after (with the exception
of 2014). When we researched the fact, we found that the crash of the
turboprop plane in 2009 prompted US federal regulations requiring more rest
and training for pilots. Given that 1,036 of our data points were US
flights, this might have been a statistically significant legal step in improving
aircraft safety.

Finally, we concluded that the general trend of decreasing fatalities in light
of the growing number of people flying everyday could imply a gradual
technological or regulatory improvement.

After initially looking at fatality rates, we wanted to take a look at
how severe the injuries were overall. Taking a look at the modified pie
chart we find that the largest groups are non-fatal and incident which
seems to indicate that though there is a high degree of accidents, the
majority do not result in any heavy casualties. In the process of making
the chart, we excluded the "unavailable" category which had a 63 count out
of 1987 and should not have materially affected the results.

Following our realization that most airline accidents do not result in
sustained injuries, we wanted to see if there was a particular phase of the
flight that is more susceptible to accidents. There was a fairly even
distribution with a particularly high likelihood of accidents in takeoff,
taxi, or landing. This is interesting because it means that interactions on
the ground lead to more accidents than in the air.

After analyzing the various distributions of accidents across these
factors, we wanted to test for if geographic might influence accident
frequency and magnitude. We plotted the latitude and longitude of every
accident on the map - what we found was that almost all of the accidents
with significant casualties were located in the US.

We determined several possibilities: 1) this might be an American
dataset or a dataset of US airline flights, 2) accidents in the US might be
better documented than elsewhere allowing more datapoints, or 3) the US
actually has the highest number of accidents in the world (which is
unlikely given regulatory environment, airplane manufacturing, etc.).
